Ctenophore Hydrodynamics and Sensory Ecology


Hydrodynamics of Mnemiopsis leidyi

Ctenophores (also known as "Comb Jellies") are gelatinous animals that are widespread throughout all of Earth's oceans. They have been observed at all latitudes, from the tropics to the poles, and at depths from the water surface to over 10,000 m. Ecologically they are some of the most important predators on the planet, consuming a variety of crustaceans as well as mollusk and fish larvae. Ctenophores are also the largest known organisms to swim using the coordinated beating of small hair-like structures called cilia. I am interested in the fluid mechanics and biomechanics that allow these strange creatures to move around and detect prey in their fluid environment.
